Phase One: Sketching Visualizations

In this section, we'll sketch some prototype visualizations that attempt to answer the question: how has the relative proportion of ages changed since 1900?

In case my handwriting is illegible, the title is "How has the relative proportion of ages changed since 1900?", the vertical axis reads "# of people", and the horizontal axis reads "Age bucket".

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • When trying to get a sense of the overall distribution of binned data like this, I typically start with a histogram, so I was thinking that being able to see the histograms for both years in the same view would give a good overview of the two distributions and allow the viewer to compare.
  • Shared axis for age bins allow for (somewhat) easy visual comparison between the two years.
  • I don't like that the visual separation of data (i.e. white space between bottom of 1900 facet and top of 2000 bars) means you have to look back and forth between the two histograms to draw any conclusions.
  • Presenting all the data in a relatively raw form allows the viewer to perform any comparison they're interested in, given enough time, but there isn't really an immediate takeaway that jumps out at you here.
  • Using same scale for vertical axes communicates the growth in total population, but also makes it more difficult to compare relative proportions.
  • Overall it seems like small multiples is nice for showing both data sets individually for an initial exploration, but it's hard to make quick comparisons.
  • For my next sketch, I'd like to see if I can come up with something where both datasets are in the same facet and also allows comparing relative proportions more easily.

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • Looking at cumulative distribution functions (CDFs) can be a quick way to answer questions like "what percentage of the population is above/below age X?" (e.g what percentage is over 75 years old, or under 20 years old?).
  • By putting both CDFs on the same plot, we can be visually drawn to points where the distributions begin to signficantly differ, as these are often points where the lines cross. For example, if the percentage of children is lower in 1900, we'll see this as the 1900 line starting below the 2000 line, and then eventually crossing as we reach adult ages.
  • A downside of this type of plot is that CDFs are typically not immediately intuitive, and typically require viewers to understand (or remind themselves of) a CDF before they can actually start parsing out interesting comparisons of the distributions.
  • Another downside is that there's not an obvious way to incorporate the sex information into this plot without making separate lines for each sex/year combination, which would result in four CDFs and a very crowded visualization.
  • While one can more quickly compare the two distributions and read off interesting summary statistics, there still isn't really an immediately digestible takeaway here.
  • For my next sketch, I'd like to combine the intuitive nature of the histogram with the ability to read off interesting quantiles from the CDF. It would also be nice to incorporte the reported sex information.

Design Rationale for Sketch 3:

  • Showing bar plots like the first sketch is a more intuitive mark that a viewer can immediately parse as a longer bar meaning a bigger number.
  • Having bar plots emanate from a common base reduces the distance that the eye has to travel to compare the two distributions.
  • Using a vertical common axis instead of a horizontal common axis helps prevent viewers from wondering if something is going on with negative numbers.
  • Using stacked bar charts makes it relatively easy to look at the sex proportion within an age bucket. Since it seems like most buckets are a 50/50 split between male and female, the main interesting takeaway is the enrichment for women at older ages, which is a coarse enough takeaway to be communicated in a stacked bar chart.
  • Adding in dashed lines for quantiles does make the chart a bit more busy, but gives some reference points for summary statistics of the distributions. Comparing the vertical position of lines also gives a quick comparison.
    • In a final version, it would be good to figure out a way to visually distinguish the 3 quartile lines (e.g. different color or stroke for 25%, 50%, 75%) to allow quick comparison of the same quartiles across the two distributions.
  • Average life expectancy is a quantity that most people have an intuitive grasp of, so showing it here can be a quick way to emphasize many more older people in 2000 compared to 1900 and provides a quickly digestible takeaway message.

Reflection

I think my sketches show a bit of an evolution of trying to pack more and more information into each plot, likely at the expense of a viewer being able to immediately grasp what's being shown. The first sketch is a bit too basic in the sense that it doesn't really show much beyond the raw data and a rough "feel" for the data. It's more of the type of exploratory plot one might generate upon first receiving a dataset. The second sketch of the CDFs provides a reflective visualization that can allow one to calculate off interesting quantities, but I think it's a bit overly technical so may be a bit confusing at first glance. The third sketch landed on a nice way to both portray the raw data in a way that facilitates visual comparison, while also preserving some interesting summary statistics. My only worry with the third sketch is that it's a bit crowded with all the annotations. For my final version, I'll likely carry forward the design from sketch 3, but try to think of ways to reduce the amount of visual clutter and also more clearly emphasize a takeaway message of an older US population in 2000 compared to 1900.

Phase Two: Final Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

Before looking at the dataset in depth, my initial goal was to provide a high-level sense of how age demographics have shifted in the 100 years between 1900 and 2000. After looking at the dataset some more (i.e. after the sketching), I thought that an interesting takeaway is how much older the population is overall. However, I felt like the dataset is fairly rich for inspection and reflection, so I also wanted to provide a full enough view of the data that a viewer could inspect and reflect on their own to ask any questions they might have.

After the sketching exercise, I felt that the third sketch provided the best balance of having a quickly parseable takeaway while also providing the rich representation that could allow a viewer to ask their own follow-up questions. Because I still felt that the sketch was a bit visually crowded, I shifted away from the quartile lines and instead decided to just show median age and life expectancy as two immediate takeaways and concrete points of comparison between the two age distributions. I also felt that it would be nice to have a concrete point of reference for just how different the US looked in 1900 vs 2000, hence the small photos of Times Square (a well-known landmark) at both timepoints.

An enumeration of other design decisions and their rationale are as follows:

  • I selected a bar chart for the core visualization, as length from a common baseline is one of the most effective encodings for comparing quantities.
  • I further made the plot into a stacked bar chart because this allows for quick evaluation of the approximate proportions of female vs male in a given age bucket, and the main takeaway of enrichment for women at older ages is strong enough to come through in this coarse representation of proportions.
  • As discussed above, having bars emanate from a common axis more quickly facilitates comparison between the two distributions without having to move your eyes back and forth across a lot of whitespace each time.
  • I selected a common horizontal scale both for practical reasons (it would be confusing to have two different scales on the same axis) and also to indirectly show population growth between the two timepoints, although I did not choose to explicitly show the total population count, as the number of annotations was already getting a bit crowded.
  • I added slight spacing between bars to visually distinguish the different age buckets and make it easier to count off the intermediate buckets without cluttering the vertical axis with a label for each bin.
  • I added vertical grid lines since the plot is very tall, so it's easy to lose track of the scale as you look up. In contrast, I removed horizontal grid lines to reduce visual clutter, as the plot is fairly narrow and the discrete nature of bins makes it a bit easier to count off what age bucket you're looking at.
  • I reduced the alpha on the bars to make them fade a bit so as to emphasize the annotations for a quick takeaway message.
  • While the standard blue/pink for male/female can be more immediately understandable by some, I felt the extra few seconds of checking a legend is worth it for the gain of not perpetuating the "shrink it and pink it" stereotype of assigning pink to women.
  • I moved the vertical axis labels to the right side both to allow the viewer to first focus on the annotations and also to make the axis labels closer to actual data due to the large amount of whitespace on the left side of the visualization.
Overall I think my design decisions served my goal of giving an immediate takeaway that the US population in 2000 is generally older than the US population in 1900, while also allowing an interested viewer to ask their own questions and parse other interesting aspects of the data (e.g. enrichment of women at older ages, larger overall population in 2000, and the growing proportion of middle-aged people in 2000). I do think that my choice of a stacked bar chart focusing on ages makes it hard to read off finer-grained information about female-male proportions, both within and across the two timepoints.

Reflection

The sketching process was definitely helpful in terms of rapidly iterating on different ideas for visualization without having to go through the nitty-gritty of getting your favorite visualization tool to actually make the plot you're imagining in your head (e.g. it likely would've taken awhile to get the CDF plot to come out correctly). The sketching phase helped me try out ways of progressively integrating more information into the visualization while also realizing that a faceted histogram was a bit too "raw", a CDF was fairly unapproachable, and that the first iteration of the horizontal stacked bar chart was too visually crowded. I think in the future, I'd like to continue with the sketching process, and try to explore a bit more "wild" types of visualizations during it, as my sketches here seemed like a bit of a linear path of increasing complexity, rather than a breadth-first exploration of many distinct types of ideas (although this may have also been driven by having the same underlying question for all the sketches).
